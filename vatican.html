Vatican City

History

Vatican City (Città del Vaticano in Italian) is an independent city state located in the heart of Rome, ruled by the Pope (Bishop of Rome). It is the centre of authority over the Roman Catholic Church. The Vatican is the smallest state in Europe. It has a total area of 0,44 km² and inside its wall live approximately 1000 people. In this small state lives the Pope, in the Apostolic palace surrounded by beautiful gardens. The palace is open to the public, but visitors have to book in advance. The Vatican City was declared independent from the rest of Italy on 11 February, 1929, with the signing of the Lateran Treaty between Pope Pius XI and Mussolini.

Top Attractions

The Sistine Chapel

Designed by Michelangelo, The Sistine Chapel is one of the most impressive treasures of the Vatican City.

Saint Peter's Basilica

One of the largest churches in the world, St Peter’s Basilica, built in Italian Renaissance style is located in the Vatican.

Saint Peter's Square

Saint Peter's Square is located in front of St. Peter's Basilica, one of the largest churces in the world.

Ten curiosities about Vatican City:

  1. Vatican City is the smallest country in the world
  2. St. Peter’s Basilica sits atop a city of the dead, including its namesake’s tomb.
  3. Caligula captured the obelisk that stands in St. Peter’s Square.
  4. For nearly 60 years in the 1800s and 1900s, popes refused to leave the Vatican.
  5. Benito Mussolini signed Vatican City into existence.
  6. Popes did not live at the Vatican until the 14th century.
  7. The Swiss Guard was hired as a mercenary force.
  8. At several times during the Vatican’s history, popes escaped through a secret passageway.
  9. The majority of Vatican City’s 600 citizens live abroad.
  10. The Vatican Observatory owns a telescope in Arizona.
